TURTLES

Our ocean waters are home to seven distinct species of sea (or marine) turtles, from the shallow seagrass meadows of the Indian Ocean to the vibrant coral reefs of the Coral Triangle and the sandy beaches of the Eastern Pacific. Sea turtles spend the majority of their lives in the ocean, even though these highly migratory animals occasionally go on land to either bask or lay their eggs. Green, hawksbill, loggerhead, leatherback, and olive ridley sea turtles are the five species on which WWF focuses its sea turtle efforts.Human actions have shifted the odds against these historic mariners' survival during the past 200 years. Sea turtles are victimized by poaching and over-exploitation as they are slaughtered for their eggs, meat, skin, and shells. They also face bycatch, or unintentional capture in fishing gear, and habitat loss.

Marine turtle are mating

In the shallow offshore seas, turtles mate primarily during the day. Others mating on the water's surface, while some turtles do so underwater. To grasp the female securely while mating, adult male turtles have strong claws. During the mating season, one female can have multiple matings with different males and store the sperm in her body. A single clutch may contain eggs that were fertilized by multiple guys since these stored sperm are used to fertilize eggs. After finding the nesting location, the female waits until dusk in the shallow waters near the shore. Because it is cooler and less visible to huge predators at night, female turtles choose to lay their eggs there.
